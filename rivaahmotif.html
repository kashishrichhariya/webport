Rivaah by Tanishq · Motif identity

A motif for
new beginnings

Designing a timeless emblem that anchors Rivaah’s experience across space, touchpoints, and narratives.

Project

Rivaah by Tanishq

Discipline

Brand motif & application system

Key Contributions

Brand exploration, concept development, motif refinement and translation across spatial and gifting touchpoints

The Brief

Create a mark that could hold the emotion of a beginning—meaningful enough for a wedding, quiet enough to belong everywhere.

For Rivaah, every detail lives alongside a life-changing choice. The motif needed to feel bridal without becoming ornamental for ornament’s sake: elegant, understated and unmistakably part of Tanishq.

The lotus became the starting point—an auspicious symbol of fertility and new beginnings. Paired with the brand mark, it offered a language that could move from architecture to the smallest object a bride carries home.

The Process

From brand meaning
to a living mark

The work moved from context to symbol, then from symbol to system—testing meaning, proportion, softness and scale before the motif entered the space.

01

Define the design language

Begin with Rivaah’s need for elegance, restraint and a symbol of new beginnings.

02

Find the shared symbol

Connect the lotus to auspiciousness, fertility and a bride’s new beginning.

03

Bring the brands together

Use the Tanishq mark as a recognisable centre within the new form.

04

Refine the gesture

Soften curves, simplify joints and hold clarity across different sizes.

05

Let the motif travel

Translate the mark through walls, leather, embroidery, metal and print.

The Symbol

The lotus,
redrawn for Rivaah

The lotus already carried the emotional meaning the project needed: it is auspicious, fertile and forever beginning again. The design asked how that familiarity could meet the Tanishq signature in a form that felt light, minimal and quietly celebratory.

Elegance Understated Minimal Auspicious New beginnings

Form Exploration

Many gestures.
One meaningful union.

The early studies tested whether the brand mark could sit beside, inside or between lotus forms. Each arrangement carried a different emotional weight—from a single bloom to a circular celebration and, finally, two lotuses meeting.

The Final Concept

Twin lotuses symbolising the union of two and new beginnings.

Material Translation

A line becomes
a tactile language

A motif only becomes part of a brand when it can survive the change of material. The form was tested through stitch, emboss, relief and metal—sometimes read as a bloom, sometimes simply felt as a quiet rhythm.

In the Space

The motif leaves the page

Scaled into the environment, the twin-lotus form becomes a soft field rather than a logo stamp. It appears in stitched wall panels, display details and gifting—holding the space together without asking to be the centre of it.

Spatial Detail

Present, but never loud

The stitched line rewards a closer look. In changing light, the motif recedes and returns—part surface pattern, part symbol, always secondary to the bride and the jewellery.

A small mark, carrying the larger story.

At the counter, the motif becomes a signature beneath the words—connecting the jewellery narrative back to the space around it.

Gifting

The detail she
takes with her

On bags and boxes, the same line changes character again. Tonal embroidery lets the motif belong to the material, while the scale shifts naturally between a generous carry bag and the smallest jewellery case.
